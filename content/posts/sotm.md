## Comfort

Trustini is my go to long distance stead. I have done a many long rides on it and know it fits me well. She is a gravel bike, relaxed geometry, buttery smooth titanium with a lot of history.

It is important to think about the contact points. The saddle, handlebars and pedals. These are where you can accumulate discomfort on the longer rides, which undealt with, can lead to damage. Everyone body is different and it is best to experiment with different setups to find out what works for you personally.

I use a narrow [Brooks C13 Cambium](https://www.brooksengland.com/en_eu/c13.html) saddle which has a bit of flex. I find it very comfortable for long rides. And team it up with a my most comfortable bib shorts from Santini that include a butt saving "C3 seatpad with anti-shock gel inserts".

For the handlebars, I use the thickest bar tape I can find. The idea is more cushion absorbs the vibrations that come through the bars better. I also wear padded gloves.

![Different width bar tapes are available.](/images/bartape.png)

My friend Micheal gave me some aero bars to try out, this gives me more hand positions to change between and hopefully avoid any nerve damage[^nerve-damage-in-bikepacking] from the long days in the saddle.

## Aerodynamics

When cycling above 15km/h, the biggest thing that is slowing you down is the air resistance or drag.

Considering I am much bigger then the bike, the biggest source of drag is me. I will try to stay as aero as possible. I will wear a tight fitting jersey and bib shorts. They don't look cool but they are practicool. Flapping in the wind is not fast.  

Wearing a helmet actually reduces drag and makes you faster, as proven by Greg LeMond in the 1989 Tour de France[^aero-helmet]. People compare helmets to see which one is fastest[^helmet-test]. So wearing a helmet is a good idea. I wear a Oakley Ar05 Race helmet which does a good job. I also use glasses with a big lens to protect my eyes from the sun, wind and bugs. The frame of the glasses matches up with the helmet and reduces drag even further. Probably minimal but hey, I will take every saving I can!

I avoided wearing a backpack, thankfully a fellow SOTM attendee offered to take my laptop with him in the bus.

The rest of my stuff was stashed in my top tube bag + frame bag + so called "Arse Rocket" saddle bag. The bags are positioned to avoid increasing the frontal area and avoid any extra turbulence. In fact the bags might even help me stay aero according to some sources[^aero-bike-bags].

![My bike](/images/bike.jpg)

It was my first time using aerobars and it was a game changer. My intention was to give me some different positions to rest my hands. But ohh boy, when I was in the areobar position, I realized they also make you significantly more streamlined and faster peircing though the air. It was also super comfortable to be able to switch between the different positions. I will definitely be using them again on long rides.

## Rolling resistance

The least rolling resistance (aka fastest) tire is typically a slick and narrow tire inflated to a high pressure.

This idea is changing became of new perspectives, lower pressures and the industry producing tubeless tires and wider rims. According to new trends a wider tire at a lower pressure can be faster then a narrow tire at a high pressure, especially on rougher surfaces.

I like Continental GP5000 tires, and according to the rolling resistance data[^rolling-resistance] the 32mm version which I am using is not much worse then the 25mm version and one of the faster tyres out there.

![Chart of different width Conti GP5000 rolling resistance](/images/width_rolling_resistance.png)

Fatter and softer tires also means more comfort, especially on the rough stuff, which is important for long rides.

I am using tubes but interested to try a tubeless setup out in the future, reported to have even lower rolling resistance according to the same site [bicyclerollingresistance.com](https://www.bicyclerollingresistance.com/specials/grand-prix-5000-s-tr-tubeless-vs-tubes). One thing I appreciate about tubes is that they are so easy to fix on the road.

## The route

I used [Komoot](https://www.komoot.com) to plan the route. It is a great tool for planning bike routes. It takes into account the surface type, elevation and traffic to give you a good idea of what to expect. I put in my start and end location and set Poznan as a waypoint to break up the journey. I adjusted the route by dragging the line though a national park to avoid the main road and that was it. I had a route.

I saved the route for offline usage on my phone, strapped the phone to the handel bars and used it to navigate.
